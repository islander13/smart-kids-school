It's our most requested camp theme, for good reason: the promise is concrete, motivating, and delivered within a week. But what does a child actually do, from Monday morning to Friday afternoon? Here's how it really unfolds.

## Day 1: the idea and the first characters

The camp starts with the most important question: what game do you want to make? A platformer, a chase game, a shooter, a maze — the child picks a direction with the teacher's help, who mainly helps frame a realistic idea for one week (initial enthusiasm sometimes aims bigger than what's feasible, and part of the teacher's job is helping prioritise). Day one usually ends with a character moving on screen, controlled by the keyboard.

## Day 2: the game rules take shape

This is the day the project starts feeling like an actual game: enemies or obstacles appear, collisions get detected ("what happens when my character touches this?"), a score or lives system shows up. It's also the day the child hits their first real logic bugs — an important moment, where the teacher guides without handing over the solution directly, so the child learns to debug on their own.

## Day 3: levels and difficulty

A single level usually isn't enough for a motivated child — they want more. Day three is often spent adding a second level, or progressively increasing difficulty (enemies move faster, there are more of them). This is where the idea of game design naturally appears: a game that's too easy is boring, one that's too hard is discouraging, and finding the right balance is already a real design question.

## Day 4: sound, scenery, polish

The final touches often make the biggest perceived difference: background music, sound effects, a polished scene, a win or lose screen. These are relatively simple additions technically, but they turn a prototype into something that really feels like a finished game — an important motivation boost heading into the last day.

## Day 5: the finished game, tested and shown off

The last day is spent on final adjustments and, most importantly, playing: the child tests their own game, sometimes invites other children in the group to try theirs, and leaves with a complete, working project they can show their family. A training certificate is handed out at the end, covering the skills practiced during the week.

## What about starting level?

No prior experience is needed — the camp uses Scratch, accessible from age 7 with no prior programming knowledge. Groups are formed by close age ranges so the dynamic stays appropriate for everyone.
