"My child is already on screens too much." It's a worry we hear often, and it comes from a good place. But it lumps together, under one word — "screen" — activities that have almost nothing in common. Watching auto-playing videos and building a game from scratch both happen in front of a screen. Yet they're two radically different experiences.

## The real problem isn't the time, it's the role you play

In front of content consumed passively — a video, a scrolling feed, a game designed to capture attention with no effort — the child is a spectator. Nothing they do changes what happens on screen. In front of a screen where they create something — a drawing, a story, a program — the child is an actor: every decision they make has a direct, visible consequence. It's this difference in posture, not the duration, that determines what the activity gives them.

## How to tell the difference, concretely

A simple sign: at the end of the activity, can your child describe what they did, or only what they saw? A child coming out of a viewing session often struggles to precisely summarise what they just watched. A child coming out of a creating session can usually explain, precisely and enthusiastically, what they built and why they made one choice over another.

## Coding is one of the few entirely active screen times

Unlike a video feed or a game designed to maximise time spent, a program does nothing on its own. Without instructions from the child, the screen stays empty. Every element — a character that moves, a rule that applies — exists because the child decided it and built it, line by line or block by block. It's structurally impossible to consume passively.

## This isn't a miracle solution, and that's not the point

This doesn't remove the need for balance: sport, playing outside, reading, time with no screen at all remain essential, and nothing replaces that. The point isn't that "coding is good, so more is better" — it's simply that not all screen hours are equal, and the distinction between consuming and creating deserves to be the real criterion, rather than the number of hours shown on a counter.
