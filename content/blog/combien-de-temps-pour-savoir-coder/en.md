"How long until my child really knows how to program?" It's a question we hear almost as often as the one about starting age, and it comes from a flawed assumption: that "knowing how to code" is a threshold you cross one day, like learning to swim. In reality it's a continuous progression, with very concrete milestones at each stage — here's what they look like.

## After a few sessions: the first blocks come to life

By the first or second session, a child can already make a character move, react to a key press, build a simple loop. It's not "programming" in the adult sense yet, but it's already the right logic: giving precise instructions to a machine and observing the result. This is the stage where the magic happens — seeing their own program react, for the first time.

## After about 3 months: a first real project, start to finish

This is usually when a child finishes their first complete project mostly on their own: a mini-game with several levels, an interactive quiz, an animation with multiple characters interacting. They start anticipating problems before they happen ("if I don't add a condition here, it'll break"), a sign that logic is settling in, not just block memorization.

## After 6 to 12 months: the transition to a real text-based language

This is the period when, for most children, Scratch starts showing its limits and the transition to Python (often via Python Turtle first) becomes natural. The child writes their first lines of text-based code, with real syntax — a symbolically important step, even though the underlying concepts remain largely the same as in Scratch.

## Beyond a year: growing independence

At this stage, a child can generally start from a personal project idea ("I want to make a game where...") and build it with less and less help, debugging their own mistakes before asking for assistance. This is also when some start getting curious about broader topics: how a website works, artificial intelligence, databases.

## Why consistency matters more than intensity

One hour a week for 6 months produces better results than six hours in a row over a single weekend. The brain needs time between sessions to consolidate what was just learned — true for coding as much as for a language or a musical instrument.
