In Geneva as elsewhere, choosing a coding class for a child is often based on a name that keeps coming up in search results, or word of mouth. That's a reasonable starting point, but not enough to really compare. Here are the concrete questions to ask any school, including ours, before committing.

## 1. What's the exact programme, and does it adapt to my child's age?

A good answer mentions a clear progression: Scratch for younger children (from age 7), a gentle transition to a text-based language like Python, with defined stages. Be wary of a vague answer like "we adapt case by case" with no further detail — individual adaptation is essential, but it needs to rest on a solid base structure.

## 2. Who teaches the classes, and based on what method?

Ask whether teachers follow a defined pedagogical method, or improvise their content session by session. With us, the programme is designed by EPFL and ETHZ graduate engineers, and teachers are trained in this method — an important distinction: it's not the individual teacher inventing the content, it's a structured programme they apply and adapt.

## 3. What's the format: one-on-one, small group, or large class?

The teacher-to-child ratio completely changes the experience. For a group camp, a maximum of 5 children per group (our standard) still lets the teacher follow each child individually; beyond that, attention dilutes quickly. For regular follow-up, one-on-one (Solo) or in pairs (Duo), explicitly ask whether the pace adapts to your child or whether they have to follow the group's.

## 4. What happens if my child doesn't take to it?

A school confident in its teaching approach usually offers some form of first-session guarantee — a refund if it's not convincing. The total absence of this option isn't disqualifying, but its existence is a good indicator of confidence.

## 5. Are the schedules flexible?

Between school, sports and homework, a single fixed weekly slot is often the first cause of dropping out. Explicitly ask about the available time window (with us, 9am to 7pm) and how flexible rescheduling actually is in case of a last-minute conflict.

## 6. Is there a concrete result at the end?

An end-of-programme certificate, a published project, something tangible: a good programme isn't measured in hours attended, it aims for a result the child can show.

## And what about in-person in Geneva?

Many Geneva families search for a class "in Geneva" thinking in-person, but for coding, most of the useful interaction (the teacher seeing the child's screen live) already happens through a screen, even in person. A well-designed 100% online format delivers on all these points without the travel constraint.
