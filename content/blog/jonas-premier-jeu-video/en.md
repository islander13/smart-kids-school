We often describe our teaching approach in general terms — progressive, adapted to the child's pace, one-on-one. Here's what that actually looks like, through the representative journey of Jonas, age 8, who discovered coding a few months ago. The narrative details are illustrative, but each stage matches a progression genuinely observed in children this age.

## Week 1: discovering Scratch, no pressure

The first session doesn't start with a theoretical explanation, but with direct hands-on work: assembling two or three blocks, making an orange cat move on screen. Jonas had never coded before. Within half an hour, he already had a character reacting to the arrow key — the basic logic (give an instruction, observe the result) was in place, without him needing to "understand programming" in the abstract sense.

## Weeks 2-3: the character becomes a real game

Once the character was under control, the teacher gradually introduced rules: an obstacle to avoid, a coin to collect, a score that increases. This is also when the first real bugs appeared — a character walking through a wall, a score that wouldn't increase. Rather than fixing it for him, the teacher guides with questions ("what is the character supposed to do here, and what is it doing instead?"), so Jonas learns to locate the problem himself.

## Week 4: difficulty and polish

A single level wasn't enough anymore — Jonas wanted "a harder game". A second level was added, with more obstacles and increasing speed. Background music and a victory screen completed the project. This is often the stage where pride becomes visible: the project no longer feels like an exercise, but like a real game someone else can play.
