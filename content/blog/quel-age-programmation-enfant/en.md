It's the question we hear most often, even before questions about price or curriculum: "What age can my child start programming?" The short answer is **from age 7**. The longer, more useful answer is that age matters less than how the subject is introduced.

## What really matters: teaching approach, not age

A 7-year-old discovering code with Scratch — a visual language where you assemble colourful blocks instead of typing text — doesn't need to type fast or understand algebra. They need curiosity, a bit of patience, and an adult who adapts the pace to their attention span rather than the other way around.

This is where a lot of generic resources found online fail: a course designed for a 14-year-old teenager will bore or discourage an 8-year-old, even if it's "technically" within reach. The reverse is also true: a teenager kept too long on very simple Scratch blocks eventually loses the interest they started with.

## The main stages, by age group

- **7-9 years old**: discovery with Scratch. The child learns basic logic (loops, conditions, events) by creating their own games and animations. The goal at this age isn't code rigor but the joy of seeing a character move because you "gave it an order".
- **9-11 years old**: a gentle transition to text-based code, often via Python Turtle, which keeps an immediate visual result (drawing shapes, patterns) while introducing real syntax.
- **11 years and up**: "pure" Python programming, with more ambitious projects — small games, calculators, simple automations. This is also the age when some children start getting curious about how the internet works, how the apps they use are built, or about artificial intelligence.

These milestones are indicative, not rigid: we have 10-year-olds very comfortable in Python and 12-year-olds who still prefer to consolidate the basics in Scratch before moving on. This is precisely why our classes are one-on-one or in small groups ([Solo and Duo plans](/en/tarifs)), rather than following a uniform school-style curriculum.

## Does a child need to already be "good at maths"?

No. This is probably the most common misconception among parents, and one of the least accurate. Programming develops logic, it doesn't require an advanced level of it beforehand. Many children actually discover logical reasoning and problem decomposition *through* code, before encountering it later in maths class — not the other way around.

What genuinely helps, at any age, is a patient teacher, a pace that leaves room to understand rather than just keep up, and the ability to ask questions without feeling judged. ## How do you know if the time has come?

Three signals are usually enough:

1. Your child asks questions about "how it works" (a video game, an app, a website).
2. They enjoy building, assembling, solving small problems (Lego, puzzles, logic games).
3. They want to try — even without knowing exactly what "programming" means.
